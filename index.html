Photos from just under a year living here.

The Anaconda Opportunity

Utah, Wyoming & Montana

Myself, Jonathan and Spencer spent two weeks hiking through what are some of the grandest locations in the world. We spent 3 days in the High Uintas Wilderness in Utah, including summiting Kings Peak, the highest point in Utah at 13,527' (4,123m) Then a brief two-night stop in Grand Teton National park which included doing the Paintbrush Divide loop. We then spent six days in the backcountry of Glacier National Park. Many altitude, such walking. Wow.

First, we start in Utah.

Utah

High Uintas Wilderness & Kings Peak

Our primary goal here was to summit Kings peak. Yet, in contrast to other times I've summited the mountain, on this fifth time for me, we wouldn't do it in one day nor only in 28 hours. We would take three days. This was to help those of us coming from lower altitude to aclimate, as well as to take in the beauty of the area, such that I now realize demands enough time to afford really taking it in.

Cirque of the Towers

Wind River Range, Wyoming

In August 2014, I had an opportunity for some mountainous adventuring just before setting off for a year of life in Nicaragua. Six friends of current and former coworkers was the group, yet the weather was looking to not be as friendly. This would be my first return to the Winds since backpacking for a little more than a week in the Lake Louise & Ross Lake area in 2008, so I was excited despite the forecast. In fact, I'm usually more excited when inclimate weather is part of the picture, so long as it isn't foolish to continue.

We had made it into the cirque, found a place to set up camp and the rain had since broken for a while, but soon it started up again. And then suddenly something which could seem unlikely in August, yet is perfectly likely even in July up in a mountain range like the winds.

Snow!

We stayed two nights in the cirque with the second night colder and windier than the first. Laying in my small 1-person tent that barely passes for much more than a bivy sack, I could hear the wind howling over the peaks for a good thirty seconds before it hit camp, betraying the contour of the bowl we were in and almost providing high enough fidelity to discern the surrounding jaggedry of the peaks themselves.
